## CAMERA ANGLE DECISION FRAMEWORK

### POWER DYNAMICS & PERSPECTIVE

#### LOW ANGLE (Camera below subject, looking up)
- **When:** Character is powerful, dominant, heroic, victorious, threatening, authoritative
- **Psychological Impact:** Appears larger, stronger, commanding, intimidating, superior
- **Use Cases:** Hero moments, triumph, authority figures, villain reveals, empowerment
- **Technical:** Camera 30-45° below eye level

#### HIGH ANGLE (Camera above subject, looking down)
- **When:** Character vulnerable, weak, defeated, overwhelmed, trapped, submissive
- **Psychological Impact:** Appears powerless, small, sympathetic, helpless, inferior
- **Use Cases:** Victim moments, despair, defeat, isolation, vulnerability
- **Technical:** Camera 30-45° above eye level

#### EYE LEVEL (Camera at subject's eye height)
- **When:** Equal power, conversation, relatability, neutral observation, intimacy
- **Psychological Impact:** Neutral perspective, natural connection, equality
- **Use Cases:** Dialogue, emotional connection, character introduction, observation
- **Technical:** Camera at subject's eye height

#### DUTCH ANGLE (Camera tilted on axis)
- **When:** Tension, unease, psychological instability, chaos, danger, disorientation
- **Psychological Impact:** Discomfort, wrongness, instability, unease
- **Use Cases:** Nightmare sequences, breakdown, suspense, conflict, madness
- **Technical:** Camera tilted 15-45° on roll axis

#### BIRD'S EYE VIEW (Directly overhead)
- **When:** Show geography, isolation, god-like perspective, scale, patterns
- **Psychological Impact:** Detachment, vastness, loneliness, omniscience
- **Use Cases:** Establishing shots, character lost in environment, surveillance feel
- **Technical:** Camera 90° above subject

---

### FRAMING & DISTANCE

#### EXTREME WIDE SHOT (EWS)
- **When:** Establish location, show scale, isolation in environment, epic scope
- **Psychological Impact:** Context, grandeur, loneliness, insignificance
- **Use Cases:** Scene openings, location changes, epic moments, world-building
- **Framing:** Subject is small in frame, environment dominates

#### WIDE SHOT (WS) / FULL SHOT
- **When:** Show character relationship to space, action sequences, full body movement
- **Psychological Impact:** Context with character, spatial awareness, action clarity
- **Use Cases:** Action, movement, environmental storytelling, character introduction
- **Framing:** Full body visible head to toe with some environment

#### MEDIUM SHOT (MS)
- **When:** Dialogue, balanced character and environment, general scenes
- **Psychological Impact:** Natural conversation framing, balanced focus
- **Use Cases:** Conversations, action setup, character interactions, transitions
- **Framing:** Waist to head visible

#### CLOSE-UP (CU)
- **When:** Emotional moments, internal state, reactions, important details
- **Psychological Impact:** Strong emotional connection, intimacy, intensity
- **Use Cases:** Revelations, processing emotions, intimate moments, reactions
- **Framing:** Face and upper shoulders, head fills most of frame

#### EXTREME CLOSE-UP (ECU)
- **When:** Intense emotion through details, important objects, micro-expressions
- **Psychological Impact:** Heightened intensity, focus on specific detail, tension
- **Use Cases:** Eyes filling with tears, trembling hands, crucial objects, intense emotion
- **Framing:** Single feature fills frame (eyes, mouth, hands, object)

---

### SPECIALTY ANGLES & TECHNIQUES

#### OVER-THE-SHOULDER (OTS)
- **When:** Conversations, showing perspective, spatial relationships between characters
- **Psychological Impact:** Viewer positioned in conversation, perspective-taking
- **Use Cases:** Dialogue scenes, confrontations, POV establishment
- **Framing:** Shoulder and back of head in foreground, other character in focus

#### PROFILE / SIDE VIEW
- **When:** Contemplation, mystery, silhouettes, parallel movement, artistic composition
- **Psychological Impact:** Artistic, mysterious, thoughtful, observational
- **Use Cases:** Walking shots, contemplation, dramatic silhouettes, transitions
- **Framing:** 90° to subject, showing side of face and body

#### POV (Point of View)
- **When:** Viewer becomes the character, subjective experience, discovery
- **Psychological Impact:** Immersion, subjectivity, first-person connection
- **Use Cases:** Discovery moments, first-person experiences, horror reveals
- **Framing:** Camera is character's eyes

#### TWO-SHOT
- **When:** Show relationship between two characters, dialogue, interaction
- **Psychological Impact:** Relationship dynamics, connection or tension
- **Use Cases:** Conversations, partnerships, conflicts between two people
- **Framing:** Two characters in frame together

#### TRACKING SHOT
- **When:** Follow movement, create energy, reveal environment progressively
- **Psychological Impact:** Momentum, journey, discovery, fluidity
- **Use Cases:** Character walking, chase sequences, exploration
- **Movement:** Camera moves with subject

## GENERATION WORKFLOW OPTIONS

### OPTION A: STANDALONE METHOD
**Best for:** Scenes with location/character changes, faster generation, more variation

1. Copy Standalone prompt for Scene 1
2. Generate Scene 1
3. Save image as `Scene_01_[Title].png`
4. Repeat independently for all scenes

**Pros:** Faster, no dependencies, easier to regenerate individual scenes
**Cons:** May have slight inconsistencies between scenes

---

### OPTION B: CONTINUITY METHOD
**Best for:** Scenes in same location with same characters, maximum consistency

1. Copy Standalone prompt for Scene 1
2. Generate Scene 1
3. Save Scene 1 image
4. For Scene 2: Upload Scene 1 image + use Continuity prompt
5. Generate Scene 2
6. Save Scene 2 image
7. For Scene 3: Upload Scene 2 image + use Continuity prompt
8. Continue chain for all scenes

**Pros:** Maximum visual consistency, cohesive look
**Cons:** Slower, errors compound, harder to fix individual scenes

---

### OPTION C: HYBRID METHOD (RECOMMENDED)
**Best for:** Most projects

1. Use Standalone for establishing shots and location changes
2. Use Continuity for consecutive scenes in same location
3. Return to Standalone when location/time changes
4. Use Continuity again for new location's consecutive scenes

**Pros:** Balance of consistency and flexibility
**Cons:** Requires more planning

## CONSISTENCY ENHANCEMENT TECHNIQUES

### For Standalone Method:
1. **Character Descriptions:** Copy exact descriptions from Module 2 into every scene
2. **Environment Keywords:** Use identical location descriptors for same setting
3. **Lighting Consistency:** Keep lighting descriptions identical within same time/location
4. **Unique Identifiers:** Repeat specific details: "scar on left eyebrow", "blue streaks in hair"
5. **Color Palette:** Use same color words: "dusty brown", "muted blue", "harsh sunlight"
6. **Texture Language:** Consistent material descriptions: "weathered leather", "rusted metal"

### For Continuity Method:
1. **Reference Quality:** Use highest quality previous image
2. **Explicit Instructions:** State "maintain exact character appearance from reference"
3. **Lighting Continuity:** Specify "same lighting as reference" if in same scene
4. **Gradual Changes:** If lighting changes, specify "gradually shifting from [previous] to [new]"

## MULTI-CHARACTER SCENE GUIDELINES

### Spatial Relationships
- **Positioning:** "Maya on left, Jax on right, 6 feet apart, facing each other"
- **Depth:** "Maya in foreground sharp focus, Jax in background slightly blurred"
- **Height Differences:** "Jax standing, Maya sitting, creating power imbalance"
- **Blocking:** "Maya between Jax and the door, blocking his exit"

### Interaction Specifics
- **Physical Contact:** "Jax's hand on Maya's shoulder, firm grip"
- **Eye Lines:** "Both looking at the radio tower in distance" or "Jax staring at Maya, Maya looking away"
- **Body Language:** "Maya pointing at equipment, Jax arms crossed, skeptical posture"
- **Proxemics:** "Intimate distance (1 foot)" or "Social distance (4 feet)" or "Public distance (12+ feet)"

### Power Dynamics Through Framing
- **Dominant Character:** Position higher in frame, closer to camera, better lit
- **Submissive Character:** Lower in frame, further from camera, in shadow
- **Equal Power:** Same height in frame, equal distance, balanced lighting
- **Shifting Power:** Start with one dominant, end with other dominant

### Focus Priority
- **Single Focus:** "Sharp focus on Maya's face, Jax soft blur in background"
- **Dual Focus:** "Both characters in sharp focus, equal importance"
- **Rack Focus:** "Start focused on Jax, shift focus to Maya mid-scene"
- **Attention Direction:** "Composition leads eye from Jax's pointing hand to Maya's reaction"

## ADVANCED TECHNIQUES

### Emotional Progression Through Camera
- **Growing Tension:** Start wide, progressively move to closer shots
- **Revelation:** Start close on reaction, pull back to reveal context
- **Intimacy Building:** Gradually move from medium to close-up
- **Isolation:** Start in group, end in extreme close-up alone

### Visual Motifs
- **Recurring Angles:** Use same angle for thematically similar moments
- **Color Coding:** Associate colors with emotions or characters
- **Symbolic Framing:** Frame characters with meaningful objects/architecture
- **Mirror Shots:** Use similar compositions for parallel moments

### Rhythm & Pacing
- **Fast Pace:** Quick cuts, varied angles, dynamic movement
- **Slow Pace:** Long holds, static camera, minimal cuts
- **Building Tension:** Gradually tighten shots, increase angle intensity
- **Release:** Return to wider, more stable shots after climax

```
Is character POWERFUL in this moment?
├─ YES → LOW ANGLE
└─ NO → Is character VULNERABLE?
    ├─ YES → HIGH ANGLE
    └─ NO → Is scene UNSTABLE/TENSE?
        ├─ YES → DUTCH ANGLE

Is this ESTABLISHING location?
├─ YES → EXTREME WIDE or BIRD'S EYE
└─ NO → What's the EMOTIONAL FOCUS?
    ├─ Environment/Action → WIDE SHOT
    ├─ Conversation/Interaction → MEDIUM SHOT
    ├─ Emotion/Reaction → CLOSE-UP
    └─ Intense Detail → EXTREME CLOSE-UP
```
